3-5 : Reversible Change and Irreversible Change

Natural change has a direction

From this point, we examine the inevitable energy losses in engines based on the principles discussed in the previous sections. Before proceeding, it is necessary to introduce an important fundamental law of nature.

● Nature changes from order to disorder

Let us again consider gas molecules within a container, this time using two containers—one containing high-temperature gas and the other low-temperature gas. When the two containers are brought into contact, heat flows from the higher-temperature container to the lower-temperature one. If left undisturbed, both will eventually reach an intermediate temperature, at which point heat transfer ceases and the system reaches an equilibrium state.

From a microscopic perspective, the container at higher temperature initially contains a greater proportion of fast-moving molecules, while the lower-temperature container contains fewer. When the two containers are brought into contact, energy is transferred through molecular interactions from the higher-temperature gas to the lower-temperature gas, increasing the average kinetic energy of the molecules in the cooler container. This process continues until both systems reach the same average kinetic energy (temperature), at which point there is no net transfer of thermal energy and the system is in equilibrium.

● No changes occur between "disorder" and "order"

The same example can be viewed from another perspective. In one container, the molecules have higher average kinetic energy, while in the other they have lower average kinetic energy, making the difference in molecular motion distinguishable. In this sense, the system contains identifiable information about its state. However, as the two containers approach equilibrium, this distinction disappears. The molecular energies become more uniformly distributed, and the system transitions to a more disordered state.

In nature, systems tend to evolve from "order" to "disorder." The reverse transformation does not occur spontaneously. For example, when a high-temperature container is placed in contact with a low-temperature container, heat flows from the hotter one to the cooler one until equilibrium is reached. The opposite process—heat flowing from a colder body to a hotter one without external work—does not occur naturally. Once the system has reached equilibrium, it cannot be returned to its exact original state without external intervention. A process for which the original state cannot be fully restored is called an irreversible process. In contrast, a reversible process is one that can, in principle, be reversed without leaving any change in the system or its surroundings.
