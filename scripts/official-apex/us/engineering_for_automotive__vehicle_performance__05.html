2-5 : Body Roll and Vehicle Motion

Utilizing body roll to adjust steering characteristic

The body of the car rolls outward during cornering. Up to this point, we have intentionally omitted the effects of body roll to simplify our discussions. However, vehicle motion is greatly influenced by body roll. Let us explore the effects of body roll in relation to overall vehicle performance.

● Effects of body roll on steering characteristic

From Fig. 2-5-2, we can observe that even if the load is doubled, the cornering force does not increase by the same amount. This is due to diminishing returns in cornering force as load increases, producing a saturation curve. During cornering, load transfers from the inner wheel to the outer wheel. As a result, the combined cornering force of the inner and outer wheels is lower than it would be without load transfer. In other words, the greater the load transfer, the smaller the total cornering force.

Fig. 2-5-2: Relationship between tire load and cornering power. Load and cornering power are not proportional; therefore, even if the load doubles, the cornering power does not double.

Fig. 2-5-3: Comparison between a car with a large amount of weight transfer and one with a smaller amount. For a car with less weight transfer, the cornering force (F1) is equal to one-half of the sum of the inner wheel (A) and outer wheel (B). For a car with greater weight transfer, the cornering force (F2) is also one-half of the sum of A' and B', but the total is reduced due to load sensitivity. A car with less weight transfer therefore produces a larger total cornering force.

● Steering characteristics when the load transfer differs between front and rear

Because the generation of cornering force varies with vertical load in a nonlinear, saturating manner, differences in load transfer between the front and rear tires will affect the steering characteristics. If the load transfer at the front wheels is greater than at the rear wheels, the vehicle will tend to understeer. If the load transfer at the front wheels is less than at the rear wheels, the vehicle will tend to oversteer.

The amount of lateral load transfer between the left and right sides is determined by the balance between the external moment generated by lateral forces and the resisting moment provided by the vehicle’s roll stiffness. This relationship is influenced primarily by front and rear roll center heights, roll stiffness distribution, and track width. In simplified terms, roll center height affects the moment generated by lateral tire forces, roll stiffness distribution governs how that moment is shared between the front and rear, and track width influences how load transfer is distributed across the tires. These relationships determine the magnitude and distribution of load transfer, helping to explain the effects of body roll on steering characteristics, as summarized in Chart 2-5-1.

Chart 2-5-1: Change in steer characteristics and suspension tuning.

Understeer increases by raising the front roll center height or by lowering the rear roll center height.
Understeer increases by raising the front roll stiffness relative to the rear.
Understeer increases by reducing the front track width or increasing the rear track width.
