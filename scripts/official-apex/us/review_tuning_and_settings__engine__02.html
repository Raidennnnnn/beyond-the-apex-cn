Overhauling the Engine

Mass-produced engines are built to tight tolerances, but not every unit operates at its absolute potential. Disassembling and rebuilding the engine with careful measurement and assembly can improve consistency and performance. During this process, lighter components can be fitted and rotating parts balanced to enhance efficiency and response. If regulations allow, displacement can also be increased, which can improve output and torque, although it may also raise mechanical and thermal loads.

Some modern engines are so finely balanced when they roll off the production line that they don’t leave a great deal of opportunity for improvement through overhauling

Increasing Displacement

Increasing engine displacement is one of the most direct ways to improve output. By increasing the amount of air-fuel mixture burned, engine performance can be enhanced. This can be done by “boring” the cylinders to a larger diameter and fitting bigger pistons, or by “stroking” the engine with a longer crankshaft throw and matching components. While both increase displacement, they affect performance differently. Increasing bore can support higher engine speeds and airflow, while increasing stroke generally improves low- and mid-range torque. However, as modern engines have become more compact and lightweight, cylinder walls are often thinner, making large increases in bore more difficult.

Balancing

In a normal engine, the weights of the pistons and connecting rods in each cylinder can vary slightly. Any imbalance in the rotating assembly, including the crankshaft, can create unwanted vibration and reduce efficiency. Balancing the engine involves disassembling it and carefully measuring each component. By matching the weight of each part and correcting the rotational balance, the engine can run more smoothly and efficiently, improving overall performance. In cases where weight correction is not sufficient, parts may be replaced altogether. This type of tuning is particularly important in one-make racing, where major modifications are not permitted.

Reducing Weight

Engine parts that move at high speed are affected by inertia, which can increase frictional losses and reduce efficiency. This can be reduced by removing material to make the components lighter. This process is often carried out together with balancing, but if parts are ground too thin, durability can be compromised.

Reinforcement

When major tuning work is carried out on an engine, the loads on each component increase, raising the risk of damage. This makes stronger parts essential, while keeping weight low is equally important. Reinforced components produced using advanced forging techniques, along with materials such as titanium alloys, offer high strength with reduced weight compared with standard parts. In racing and heavily tuned engines, forged aluminum pistons and, in some cases, titanium connecting rods are used to meet these demands.
