Construction and Principles

The suspension is the system between the body and the wheels that absorbs shocks from the road while supporting the vehicle. It also plays a key role in handling, making it a critical part of a car’s design.

Suspension systems are broadly classified as dependent or independent. In a dependent system, movement of one wheel affects the other on the same axle. In an independent system, the left and right wheels move separately. Dependent layouts include beam or live axles, while independent designs include strut-type and double wishbone suspension.

The suspension is made up of springs, dampers and linkages. The springs absorb impacts from the road, while the dampers control spring motion to maintain ride comfort and stability. The linkages guide wheel movement, helping the tires maintain consistent contact with the road. Together, the system supports the vehicle and manages how forces are transmitted through the tires.

In this design, the strut assembly serves a structural role, effectively replacing the upper control arm found in a double wishbone setup. This reduces component count and helps free up space in the engine bay.

Springs

Springs absorb impacts from the road and help maintain a consistent ride height. They play a key role in controllability, and have a major influence on handling and stability. Spring tuning alone can significantly affect a car’s performance. Most vehicles use steel coil springs, although some are equipped with air suspension systems.

Shock Absorbers

A coil spring can absorb an impact under load, but once compressed, it will continue to oscillate. Dampers control this motion. The most common type uses resistance generated by a piston moving through oil, often with gas pressure to improve performance. By slowing the spring’s movement, the damper reduces unwanted vertical motion. Like springs, dampers play a key role in control and stability.

Suspension Arms

Suspension arms control wheel movement. They connect the wheel assembly to the body or subframe through bushings. Common types include A-arms, or wishbones. They are typically made from steel, although aluminum is often used in performance applications to reduce weight. In layouts with two arms per wheel, such as double wishbone suspension, they are referred to as upper and lower control arms.

Suspension Bushings

Suspension bushings are compliant components fitted at the joints of suspension links and arms, as well as other mounting points on the body. If they are too soft, they can deform under load during cornering, introducing unwanted movement and reducing control and stability. For this reason, bushings are typically made from rubber compounds that balance compliance and durability. In competition cars, rigid spherical joints, often called pillow-ball joints, are used instead to maximize precision. Bushings play an important role in allowing the springs and dampers to function as intended.

Sway Bars/Stabilizers

A sway bar, or stabilizer, is a device that reduces body roll by using the torsional resistance of a bar connecting the left and right suspension. It is also known as an anti-roll bar. The bar links both sides of the suspension and primarily reacts to differences in movement between them. When cornering, the outside suspension compresses while the inside extends. The sway bar resists this difference, helping to limit roll and stabilize the vehicle. By adjusting its stiffness, it can also be used to influence understeer and oversteer balance.
