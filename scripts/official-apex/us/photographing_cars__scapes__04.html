Avoid Making Cars Look Unnaturally Distorted When Using Wide Lenses

I use a range of different lenses when photographing cars. In the past, I basically thought that if I wanted to take good car photos, I had to use a telephoto lens. Things changed when Audi produced a catalogue that featured photos taken with a wide lens — I think it was some time in the 1980s. The shots in that catalogue looked great, and that really revolutionized things for me. Car designs have also changed a lot recently, and I think there are now more and more shapes and styles that suit wide-lens photography.

Having said that, there’s definitely a knack to using wide-angle lenses such as the 35, 28 and 24mm. Wide lenses are convenient because they can capture a lot of content in a single image. The downside is that it’s easier for photographs to become distorted due to the compressed perspective. While there are some cases when you’ll want to deliberately introduce distortion, in car photography, you’ll generally need to be careful that you don’t create any unnecessary warping of your subject.

Here is the same ‘Scapes’ background seen from two different angles. In the left image, emphasis is placed on the front of the car, making it seem as though it is bursting out of the image.
In the right image, the area around the car has been trimmed and the car has been placed deeper into the image, allowing its body-line to be displayed crisply and clearly.

The bigger and closer to the foreground a car is, the more likely it is to become distorted. As such, a good rule of thumb when shooting with a wide-angle lens is to allow plenty of background to appear on both sides, placing it in a position where it takes up no more than 1/3 of the width of the frame. Of course, a good way to create stable compositions is to shoot the car directly from the side or the front. Even in these cases, the 1/3 rule can often still prove useful.

The problem of distortion is an issue in ‘Scapes’ just as it is in real life. While many of the ‘Scapes’ backgrounds have been shot using telephoto lenses, there are also quite a few that were shot with wide lenses. This being the case, you’ll need to take care to avoid unwanted distortion.

As long as you can do this, there are plenty of interesting wide-angle techniques to try. Trimming allows you to cut out unnecessary parts of an image, giving you the freedom to focus only on a single area, as though you were using a telephoto lens. When placing a car against a background, you mustn’t let the background ‘win out’ over the car. Make good use of trimming to give the car a sense of presence and you’ll be able to create photos with real impact. Use this technique and the many others available in ‘Scapes’ and you’ll soon be taking even better photos than me!
