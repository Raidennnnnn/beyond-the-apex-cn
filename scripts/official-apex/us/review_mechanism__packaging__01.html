Dimensions

A vehicle’s frame and the fundamental layout of its components are among its most critical specifications. Because these are determined in the early stages of development, they are the most difficult to change later. These core design choices have a major influence on the three primary functions of a vehicle: acceleration, handling, and braking. Any shortcomings in these areas are difficult to overcome through tuning alone, and even small differences in the base design can lead to significant variations in performance. In addition, the effectiveness of any tuning is strongly dependent on the vehicle’s underlying potential. To fully maximize performance, it is important to understand how these fundamental specifications shape the way a car drives.

Wheelbase

The wheelbase is the distance between the center of the front wheels and the center of the rear wheels, viewed from the side. This dimension has a significant influence on a vehicle’s behavior. A longer wheelbase generally improves straight-line stability and reduces sensitivity to road undulations and crosswinds. It also tends to provide a smoother, more comfortable ride. In contrast, a shorter wheelbase enhances steering responsiveness and agility, making the vehicle more maneuverable, particularly in tight corners, though at the expense of stability.

Overhang

The front overhang is the distance from the center of the front wheels to the foremost end of the front bumper. The rear overhang is the distance from the center of the rear wheels to the end of the rear bumper. Mass located in these overhang areas increases the vehicle’s yaw moment of inertia—its resistance to rotation—thereby reducing maneuverability. For this reason, heavier components are ideally positioned within the wheelbase, closer to the vehicle’s center. This is particularly important for major masses such as the engine. That said, some overhang is necessary for packaging and aerodynamic performance, so it cannot be eliminated entirely.

Track Width

Track width refers to the distance between the left and right wheels on an axle. Increasing track width generally improves lateral stability and grip during cornering, as it widens the base over which the vehicle’s weight is distributed. This reduces lateral load transfer and allows the tires to maintain better contact with the road surface, improving traction. Increasing the track width of the driven wheels can therefore enhance the vehicle’s ability to transmit power effectively.

A narrower track width, on the other hand, can make a vehicle feel more responsive but typically reduces overall stability. It is also common—especially in performance and race cars—for the front and rear track widths to differ in order to fine-tune handling balance.

Height

Height is measured from the road surface to the highest point of a vehicle. In general, a lower vehicle height corresponds to a lower center of gravity, which reduces body roll during cornering and can improve handling performance and cornering speed. However, reducing height also introduces trade-offs. It limits cabin space, reduces available suspension travel (the range over which the suspension can compress and extend), and increases the risk of the vehicle bottoming out over bumps or uneven surfaces.

Weight

Weight is a crucial factor in determining vehicle performance. The lighter the car, the less load is placed on the engine, allowing more of its power to be used for acceleration. Additional benefits include reduced braking demand and improved cornering performance due to lower inertia. The ratio of a car’s weight to its maximum power output is known as the power-to-weight ratio. A lower ratio generally results in quicker acceleration and a more responsive driving feel. Vehicle weight also has a significant impact on fuel efficiency and emissions. As a result, reducing weight has become a key focus in modern automotive design, both for performance and environmental considerations.
