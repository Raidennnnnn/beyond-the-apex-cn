Brake Discs and Calipers

● Types of Brake Disc

Solid Discs

The solid disc is the most basic type, consisting of a simple metal rotor. Its heat dissipation is lower than that of a ventilated disc, but its low cost means it is often used on rear brakes and in lower-load applications. All brake discs must withstand high temperatures and dissipate heat effectively, which is why most are made from cast iron.

Ventilated Discs

Ventilated discs use a two-piece internal structure, with vanes between the friction surfaces to allow airflow. Originally developed for racing, they are now common on production cars. Compared with solid discs, they run cooler, improving resistance to fade and extending brake pad life. The trade-off is slightly increased weight due to the thicker construction.

● Advanced Ventilated Disc Types

Cross Drilled Discs

Cross-drilled discs are similar to ventilated discs, but with holes drilled through the rotor surface to aid cooling and gas evacuation. They are often used on high-performance road cars and in some racing applications. The holes can also help clear water and brake dust from the contact surface. Another variation, the slotted disc, uses machined grooves to achieve a similar effect.

Spiral Fin Discs

Spiral-vane ventilated discs use internal cooling vanes shaped in a curved or spiral pattern between the friction surfaces. These vanes are designed to optimize airflow through the disc as it rotates, improving heat dissipation. As a result, cooling efficiency is enhanced under sustained braking. This type of disc is commonly used in high-performance cars and heavier, high-powered sedans.

● Types of Calipers

Floating

A floating caliper uses a piston on one side to press a brake pad against the disc. The caliper then slides on its mounts, pulling the pad on the opposite side into contact. This design automatically equalizes pad contact with the disc and provides consistent braking feel. Floating calipers are compact and lightweight, and can maintain effective braking even with minor disc deformation. While they are not ideal for sustained high-performance use, they are well suited to everyday driving.

Opposing Piston

This setup uses pistons on both sides of the disc to clamp the pads evenly from each side. Because this design makes the caliper larger and heavier, it is often made from aluminum to reduce weight, which requires careful engineering to maintain rigidity. It is highly effective in demanding conditions such as circuit driving, but to make full use of its performance, high-quality discs and proper cooling are essential. Multi-piston designs—four- and six-piston calipers with larger pad areas—are now common on performance road cars. A large opposed-piston caliper visible behind the wheels is often a sign of a car’s performance focus.
