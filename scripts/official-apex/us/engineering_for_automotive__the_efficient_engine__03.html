3-3 : Carnot’s Conclusion

Surprising abstraction of the heat engine

● Only two temperatures of the heat reservoir determines efficiency

It is generally accepted that the Carnot Cycle represents the maximum possible efficiency for a heat engine operating between two temperatures. Carnot’s key insight was that, in an ideal cycle, heat transfer occurs only between bodies at the same temperature, avoiding losses associated with temperature differences. For this reason, the Carnot Cycle serves as the benchmark for an ideal engine. Another important result is that the efficiency of this cycle depends only on the temperatures of the high- and low-temperature reservoirs. ● The ultimate abstraction of the heat engine

The relationship between the theoretical efficiency of the Carnot Cycle and the temperatures of the heat reservoirs has been established, but Carnot’s conclusion also highlights an important engineering insight. As the equation shows, the theoretical efficiency of the Carnot Cycle depends only on the temperatures of the high- and low-temperature reservoirs; it does not depend on the engine’s structure or the working fluid, whether air, steam or any other substance. In other words, the Carnot Cycle is governed by fundamental thermodynamic limits rather than by the specific design of an engine.

The Carnot heat engine isolates the essential processes of heat transfer and work from the many complexities of a real engine, thereby providing an abstract model of a heat engine. It captures all the elements necessary to describe ideal performance, without unnecessary detail. In this sense, it represents a complete and highly refined abstraction.

In 1824, Sadi Carnot published a technical essay titled Reflections on the "Motive Power of Fire," in which he presented the concept of the Carnot Cycle. His aim was to encourage improvements in steam engines from an engineering perspective.

At the time, steam engines were widely used and undergoing rapid development. Steamships had already crossed the Atlantic only a few years before Carnot’s work. However, little attention had been given to the underlying scientific principles of engine operation. As a result, improvements were often based on practical experience and empirical judgment. Carnot set out to examine the relationship between heat engines and fundamental physical principles, rather than focusing solely on engine structure or mechanical design.

The importance of Carnot’s work was not immediately recognized. In 1832, during a cholera outbreak, he contracted the disease and died at the age of 36. At the time, it was common practice to destroy the belongings of those who had died from cholera, and many of Carnot’s papers were lost.

After his death, his ideas were further developed and promoted by Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron. This led to wider recognition of Carnot’s theories and established him as a pioneer of thermodynamics.

Note that William Thomson, who expressed the theoretical efficiency of the Carnot Cycle in mathematical form, also established the concept of absolute temperature through this derivation.
