Aerodynamics

● Downforce

Improving High-Speed Performance

At high speeds, aerodynamic effects become a major factor. These can broadly be divided into two areas: drag, which limits top speed, and lift, which reduces tire load by acting to raise the car. The two are closely related, as changes that reduce drag can also increase lift, while measures that reduce lift often add drag. Achieving the right balance between them is essential.

A key aspect of high-speed tuning is the management of downforce. Downforce is generated by aerodynamic components that create pressure differences, pushing the car toward the road and increasing tire load. While increased downforce improves stability and cornering performance—particularly in high-speed turns—it also adds drag and can reduce top speed. Conversely, reducing downforce lowers drag and increases straight-line speed, but at the expense of cornering grip and stability.

The required level of downforce depends on the nature of the circuit. Although it may seem beneficial to run high downforce from the outset, this does not always produce the best results. A common approach is to begin with relatively low downforce and increase it gradually based on the demands of high-speed corners. For low-power cars, prioritizing straight-line speed by limiting excess downforce can often be more effective.

Adjusting the balance of front and rear downforce also influences handling at high speed. Increasing front downforce raises front grip and tends to reduce understeer, promoting a more responsive turn-in, while increasing rear downforce improves rear stability and tends to reduce oversteer. Fine adjustments of aerodynamic balance can have a significant impact on performance, particularly on high-speed circuits.
