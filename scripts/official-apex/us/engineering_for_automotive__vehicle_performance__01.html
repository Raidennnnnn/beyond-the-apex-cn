2-1 : Tire Dynamics

Understanding the forces created by the tire

● Cornering force

The type of force that deforms an object in a shearing manner is called shear force, and the material property that resists this deformation is called the shear modulus. When a tire is subjected to lateral forces, it deforms in shear as shown in Fig. 2-1-1. At the same time, the shear stiffness of the tire resists this deformation. This resisting behavior of the tire generates the forces required for the car to accelerate, decelerate and turn.

Fig. 2-1-1: Cross-section diagram illustrating tire deformation and force. In general, greater lateral stiffness results in a larger cornering force.

Fig. 2-1-2 is a sectional illustration of a tire during cornering. As shown, there is a difference between the orientation of the tire and the direction of travel of the vehicle. In other words, force is generated through the interaction between the tire and the road surface as the tire both rotates and deforms laterally. The angle between the tire’s orientation and the direction of travel is called the slip angle. The force acting perpendicular to the direction of travel is called cornering force. A vehicle is able to turn because of the cornering force generated by the tire.

In general, if the tire’s lateral stiffness is higher, a greater cornering force can be produced for the same slip angle. However, if the stiffness is too high, the tire can reach its grip limit at a small slip angle, which may result in less progressive feedback to the driver. If the stiffness is too low, the tire will deform excessively, which can make the vehicle feel unstable and reduce driver confidence.

Fig. 2-1-2: Top view of a tire illustrating the relationship between deformation and force. Lateral force acts perpendicular to the direction of travel. Cornering force refers to the lateral force generated by the tire due to slip angle.

● Relation between cornering force and slip angle

Fig. 2-1-3 shows the relationship between slip angle and cornering force. When the slip angle is small, the cornering force increases approximately linearly. However, as the slip angle becomes larger, the cornering force begins to saturate. The rate of change of cornering force with respect to slip angle is called cornering power. A tire that generates a large cornering force at a small slip angle can be said to have high cornering power.

Fig. 2-1-3: Correlation between slip angle and cornering force. Cornering power is high in the range where the slip angle is relatively small. As the slip angle increases, the cornering force begins to saturate and the rate of increase diminishes.

● Air pressure and cornering power

In general, when tire pressure is relatively low, increasing the pressure raises the tire’s lateral stiffness, which can increase cornering power. However, increasing pressure also reduces the contact area of the tire. As pressure rises, these two effects oppose each other. At low vertical loads, the reduction in contact area due to higher pressure is more significant than the increase in stiffness, resulting in a decrease in cornering power. On the other hand, at higher vertical loads, the increase in stiffness becomes dominant, leading to an increase in cornering power. To maximize cornering power, the characteristics of the tire and the applied load must be carefully considered and balanced.

● Lateral force of the tire associated with driving and braking

Viewed from above, the force generated perpendicular to the tire’s direction of travel is called lateral force. It is important to understand how this lateral force is related to the driving and braking of the vehicle. When the accelerator or brake is applied, the tire’s available grip is shared between longitudinal force (driving or braking) and lateral force. As a result, the amount of lateral force that can be generated at a given slip angle is reduced. This relationship is illustrated in Fig. 2-1-4. Although it is often referred to as a friction circle, the diagram is typically oval in shape rather than perfectly circular, since longitudinal and lateral forces influence the tire differently. Because acceleration and braking are continuously combined in racing conditions, the tire’s ability to generate force in combined directions has a significant effect on lap times.

Fig. 2-1-4: Friction circle. The cornering force of the tire is influenced by slip angle. The oval perimeter represents the limit of combined tire force, including both lateral and longitudinal components.
