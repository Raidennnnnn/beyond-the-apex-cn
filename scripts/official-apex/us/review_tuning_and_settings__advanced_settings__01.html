High-Speed Circuits, Technical Courses

● High-Speed Circuits

Improving Top Speed

For high-speed circuits, the goal is to maintain stability through fast corners. This typically involves using relatively stiff suspension and a low ride height. However, if the ride height is set too low and suspension travel is reduced, the car may struggle to absorb bumps and surface undulations, reducing overall grip. When using stiffer springs, slightly softer anti-roll bars can allow some body movement to help maintain tire contact. Conversely, if the springs are softened to cope with uneven surfaces, increasing anti-roll bar stiffness can help control excess roll. In this way, anti-roll bars can be used to fine-tune the balance of the suspension.

Wheel alignment is also an important factor. Increasing rear toe-in can improve straight-line stability and high-speed control. A small amount of negative camber is typically used to maintain grip in corners, but excessive camber should be avoided, as it can reduce the effective contact patch during braking and straight-line driving.

As with any setup, the goal of gearing is to keep the engine within its powerband as much as possible. The final drive should be selected so that top gear approaches the redline near the end of the longest straight. In terms of aerodynamics, downforce should be kept as low as practical to maximize straight-line speed, while still maintaining sufficient stability for cornering and braking.

● Technical Courses

Efficiently Transfering Power to the Road

For a technical course with many tight corners, the aim is to achieve quick turn-in and strong acceleration out of bends with minimal loss of momentum. The first step is to set the ride height appropriately for the surface, keeping it as low as possible without compromising suspension travel or causing instability.

To improve rotation, the front springs can be softened and the rear springs stiffened, with more moderate changes in rear-wheel-drive cars. Damper settings should be adjusted with the same objective. In terms of alignment, a small amount of front toe-out is often used to sharpen initial turn-in, while reducing it can improve stability through and beyond the apex. Negative camber should be used in moderation to maintain traction during braking and cornering.

Transmission gearing should favor a close-ratio setup to maintain engine speed rather than prioritizing top speed, with a shorter (numerically higher) final drive to improve acceleration.

If extensive engine tuning is possible, the focus should be on strengthening low- and mid-range torque to improve acceleration out of corners, rather than maximizing peak power at high rpm. Aerodynamic settings should favor higher levels of downforce at both the front and rear to support stability and grip in cornering, rather than outright top speed.
