Photographing Cars 7

Looking at Photos You’ve Taken is the Best Way to Learn

I hope the points I’ve discussed so far have made you keen to try your hand at car photography. I’d like to finish by sharing a few additional tips to help turn that enthusiasm into talent!

The first thing I’d like to say is that, when you feel that you like something, it’s really important to go with that feeling.

The ‘Scapes’ feature includes photos from countless global locations taken by myself and the team. Begin by taking a look through these photos and finding one you like. The simple act of choosing one image out of so many will already set you on the path to improving your photographic sensibilities. You don’t need to think
about it too deeply, just go with your gut! It doesn’t matter if it’s somewhere you want to visit or if you think the scenery is nice: any reason is a good reason. The point is just to choose something to get you started. And once you’ve taken your first photo, and then your tenth, and then your twentieth, your own unique artistic voice
will begin to emerge. The photos you create will start to reflect your tastes and preferences, and to exhibit your own particular flavor. Understanding this is a hugely important part of the process of becoming a better photographer.

My second piece of advice is that there’s no better learning material than the photos you’ve taken yourself. This doesn’t mean that you shouldn’t look at photos that others have taken. In fact, I’d recommend that you look at as many wonderful pictures as you can, just as I’d like you to watch good movies and listen to beautiful music. Once you’ve done that, however, it’s important to look back at the photos you’ve taken yourself. Examine shots you took six months ago, or even a year. You should notice that some things now feel unnatural to you. ‘Maybe the car should be a bit smaller,’ you might think, or ‘Maybe it should be a little more to the left’. When you get that kind of feeling, be sure to revisit the same background and try to retake the photo. One of the good things
about ‘Scapes’ is that you can go back and take a photo at exactly the same moment: the same time of day, the same lighting conditions, everything. I am convinced that revisiting your old work in this manner is the key to becoming a better photographer.

Should there be a contest for photographs taken in ‘Scapes' in the future, I’m looking forward to seeing works that far exceed anything I could manage in terms of imagination and technical ability. The world of photography is endlessly deep, so go ahead and dive in! Doing so will allow you to see for yourself just what an amazing tool ‘Scapes’ is.

Chikara Kitabatake
Born in 1957, Chikara Kitabatake attended photography school and then joined the photo department of a publishing company at the age of 20. After spending time as legendary car photographer Haru Tajima’s apprentice, he became a motorsports photographer himself, travelling to various countries to photograph events such as the F1 Grand Prix and building up a wealth of experience and know-how in the process. Later, he focused on taking photos for car catalogues and cover shots for motoring magazines. He has a deep connection to the Gran Turismo series, and took many of the location photos that are used in ‘Scapes'.
