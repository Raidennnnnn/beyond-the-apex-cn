Adjusting Handling Characteristics

Tuning the suspension system for sports driving often involves sacrificing some comfort for performance. On smooth surfaces such as a race circuit, lowering the ride height reduces the center of gravity and can improve stability. Stiffer suspension reduces unwanted body movement during acceleration, braking and cornering, helping maintain sharper handling. However, if the suspension is too stiff, it will not absorb load transfer effectively and overall grip and drivability will suffer. The ideal setup balances stiffness with the need for weight transfer. Depending on the vehicle and surface, a softer setup may sometimes provide better traction.

Shock Absorbers

The objective of upgrading shock absorbers is to provide increased damping compared with standard units, which are often tuned for ride comfort. Higher damping allows the car to maintain stability under high speeds and heavy loads, improving overall control. Shock absorber upgrades should generally be carried out in conjunction with spring tuning to ensure a balanced suspension setup.

Springs

Springs improve handling by supporting the vehicle and influencing ride height, which affects the center of gravity. They also play an important role in maintaining stability by controlling body movement during cornering, braking and acceleration, helping to limit roll, nose-dive and squat.

Height-Adjustable Suspension

Height-adjustable suspension allows the ride height to be changed independently of the springs, while also providing adjustable damping through the shock absorbers. This makes it possible to fine-tune the setup to suit different conditions. There are several methods of adjusting ride height, including threaded collars, C-rings and bracket-type mounts.

Anti-roll Bars

Increasing the stiffness of the anti-roll bars can further reduce body roll during cornering. A stiffer front anti-roll bar tends to increase understeer, while a stiffer rear bar tends to promote oversteer.

Bushings

Using stiffer bushings in dampers, suspension links and other mounting points can reduce unwanted movement and improve steering response and handling precision. Suspension bushings are typically made from elastomer materials such as rubber or polyurethane, though spherical “pillow-ball” joints are also used, employing a metal bearing to allow more precise movement.
