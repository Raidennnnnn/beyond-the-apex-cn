Ensuring safe and enjoyable races

Gran Turismo 7's Sport mode allows you to compete against other players in online races. In these races, consideration of others is paramount. Motor racing is a contact sport that takes place in cars, but drivers can enjoy safe races by being aware of each others' presence.

The key points of racing etiquette are listed below. Beginners should pay particularly close attention!

● Don't drive off the track

When racing, you can only drive on the approved track surface. With the exception of certain tracks, such as urban circuits, white lines are painted on both sides of the track, and you should try to drive within these lines at all times. In most races you will incur a penalty if all four of your tires leave the track.

When you're involved in a tightly fought race against your rivals, it's easy to get carried away and attempt to cut a corner. Try to avoid doing this, and maintain a fair racing line at all times.

Be sure to drive between the white lines on the track. You will be penalized if all four of your wheels leave the area between the white lines.

● Don't block other cars when returning to the track

If you misjudge the braking point, you might find yourself driving off the track. You'll want to return to the track right away, of course, but be aware that blocking—or colliding with—other cars as you do so will incur a penalty.

Check whether there are cars behind you and ensure that you return safely to the track. If you're still getting used to racing, there is a 'Driver Assistance' feature that will automatically replace your car on the track.

Take care not to obstruct any of the cars behind you when returning to the track.

● Smoothly yield the racing line

If there's a faster car coming up from behind, you should yield the racing line. Refusing to do so by abruptly changing your line and obstructing chasing cars is dangerous, and could lead to collisions.

Get into the habit of checking your rear view mirror regularly, and smoothly yield the racing line to any faster cars that are coming up from behind. In 'Touring Car' races, you can use your indicators to show other drivers that you are changing your line.

When a car that's clearly faster approaches from behind, be sure to smoothly yield the racing line. It's a good idea to use your indicators to show that you're moving out of the way.

● You can only change course to defend your position once

If a rival car is closing in from behind, you can change your course in order to protect your position—but only once. Repeatedly changing course in order to prevent them from overtaking is a violation, and will incur a penalty.

When returning to the racing line after changing course, be sure to leave one car's width of space between you and the side of the track. This is to allow rival cars the opportunity to overtake.

You can only change your line to block cars coming up behind you once.

● Running other cars off the track is forbidden

Out-in-out is the fundamental principle of cornering, but there are times when it isn't appropriate. For example, if you're overtaking a rival car on the outside on a corner. Moving to the inside of the corner at the apex would block off the rival car, driving them off the track. As such, you must leave one car's width of space between you and the inside of the corner when performing such a maneuver.

If there's a rival car on your inside at a the corner, driving close to the inner edge of the corner so as to force them off the track is bad etiquette. Be sure to always leave one car's width of space.

● Following the line when entering and exiting the pit lane

Be aware that entering and exiting the pit lane while racing can get in the way of other cars. Suddenly swerving into the pit lane at the last minute, or driving straight out of the pit lane and into the main racing line, will incur a penalty.

There are white lines on the track to guide you in and out of the pit lane. Be sure to follow these lines when taking a pit stop.

Suddenly swerving into the pit lane is very dangerous. Follow the white lines on the track which guide you into the pit lane.

● Pay attention to yellow and blue flags

In motorsports, flags are used to communicate things to drivers, such as the fact that there are hazards on the track. There are various types of flags, but the most important ones for beginners are the yellow and blue flags.

The yellow flag indicates that there is a hazard on the track, and is waved at the post immediately before the hazard. Drivers must slow down in the sector where the flag is waved, and overtaking is forbidden. Inadvertently overtaking rival cars that have slowed down incurs a penalty, so be careful. A green flag is used to indicate that the track is clear and that the restrictions are no longer in place.

The blue flag is used to inform a driver that a faster car is coming up behind them, and is used to indicate to cars that they're about to be lapped. When you see the blue flag, try to let the faster car pass you as smoothly as possible. If you continually refuse to yield, you will incur a penalty.

A yellow flag is being waved on the post on the right. You must reduce your speed immediately and refrain from overtaking. A green flag indicates that these restrictions have been lifted.
