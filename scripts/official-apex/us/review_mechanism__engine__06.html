Performance Keywords

When reviewing a vehicle’s catalog specifications, you’ll encounter a range of figures and technical terms. Understanding these is essential to grasp a car’s characteristics and potential.

Five core terms define an engine’s capability. You may recognize terms such as “horsepower” and “torque,” but a closer look clarifies what they mean for a vehicle’s performance.

Horsepower

Horsepower represents the upper limit of an engine’s output and is usually expressed as “PS” or “hp.” One PS (metric horsepower) equals the power required to lift a 75-kilogram mass one meter in one second. In other words, a 100-PS engine could lift about 1 ton 7.5 meters in one second at maximum output. Horsepower is calculated from torque and engine speed, so a low-displacement engine can still produce strong output if it revs high enough. Internationally, output is also listed in kilowatts (1 PS = 0.735 kW).

Torque

Torque is a measure of turning force. For example, applying 1 kilogram of force to a nut with a 1-meter-long wrench produces 1 kilogram-meter of torque. In an engine, torque describes the force acting to turn the crankshaft.

The higher the torque, the greater the force sustaining engine speed, and the easier it is for the driver to control the engine.

Displacement/Number of Cylinders

Displacement describes the volume of the air-fuel mixture drawn into an engine. In a reciprocating engine, it is calculated by multiplying the swept volume of one cylinder by the number of cylinders.

Greater displacement generally brings higher output, but as the volume of each cylinder increases, engine rotation becomes less smooth. That is why engines often use more cylinders to keep individual cylinder volume low. Increasing cylinder count also raises the number of combustion events per crankshaft rotation, resulting in smoother operation.

Generally speaking, a cylinder’s displacement falls between 350cc and 600cc. However, engines with a large number of cylinders are more expensive, so cylinder size is often dictated by the vehicle’s size and class.

Bore/Stroke Ratio

The bore/stroke ratio is the relationship between the diameter of the cylinder and the length of the piston’s movement within the cylinder. Engines with a ratio of less than 1:1 are known as long-stroke engines, while those with a ratio of more than 1:1 are short-stroke. A ratio of exactly 1:1 is known as square. The bore/stroke ratio affects how the engine behaves.

Generally speaking, a long-stroke engine can produce strong torque at low to medium revs, while high-rev power is harder to achieve. The opposite is generally true of a short-stroke engine.

Compression Ratio

Compression ratio describes how much the air-fuel mixture is compressed inside the cylinder, and it has a strong influence on engine output.

It is calculated by dividing the cylinder volume with the piston at bottom dead center (total volume) by the volume with the piston at top dead center (combustion chamber volume). Total cylinder volume is the displacement plus the combustion chamber volume.

For example, in a 2,000cc four-cylinder engine, displacement per cylinder is 500cc. If the combustion chamber volume is 50cc, total cylinder volume is 550cc. Dividing 550cc by 50cc gives a compression ratio of 11:1.

Typically, naturally aspirated engines have compression ratios between about 9:1 and 11:1. Higher ratios generally produce more output for a given displacement. Engines with forced induction often use lower compression ratios, typically in the 7:1 to 9:1 range.
